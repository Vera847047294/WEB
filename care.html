Terrarium Care Guide

Everything you need to keep your micro-ecosystem thriving

The Science of Closed Terrariums

A closed terrarium is a miniature ecosystem in a glass container. The magic happens when sunlight enters the sealed environment, triggering photosynthesis. Plants release moisture through transpiration, which condenses on the glass walls and falls back into the soil—creating an infinite water cycle that sustains the entire system.

This self-sustaining cycle means your terrarium only needs attention once every 3-6 months. It's nature's perfect design, captured in glass.

Light drives photosynthesis, the engine of the ecosystem

Water cycles continuously through transpiration and condensation

Air is recycled as plants release oxygen and absorb CO2

Soil nutrients feed roots and support beneficial microorganisms

Care by Terrarium Type

Specific guidance for your unique ecosystem

Forest Terrariums

Mosses, ferns, and woodland plants

  • Indirect light only—direct sun will overheat and scorch
  • Water sparingly: once every 4-6 months
  • Watch for condensation—should fog lightly, not drip
  • Trim any dead leaves promptly to prevent mold
  • Moss may brown slightly in winter—this is normal
  • Keep away from heating vents and cold drafts

Rainforest Terrariums

Tropical ferns, orchids, and humidity lovers

  • Bright, indirect light—mimic dappled forest canopy
  • Water every 3-4 months—tropical plants need more moisture
  • Heavy condensation is normal and desired
  • Monitor for mold on leaf surfaces—increase airflow if present
  • Orchid blooms last 2-4 weeks, then drop naturally
  • High humidity environments like bathrooms are ideal

Desert Terrariums

Succulents, cacti, and drought-tolerant plants

  • Can tolerate more direct sunlight than other types
  • Water rarely: every 6-12 months is sufficient
  • Minimal condensation is ideal—desert plants hate wet roots
  • If condensation becomes excessive, open lid for 24 hours
  • Cacti may grow slowly but should remain firm and vibrant
  • Perfect for sunny windowsills and dry environments

Frequently Asked Questions

Quick answers to common terrarium questions

Closed terrariums typically need watering only once every 3-6 months, depending on the type. Forest terrariums every 4-6 months, rainforest every 3-4 months, and desert terrariums can go 6-12 months. The key indicator is condensation—if you see heavy dripping, wait longer. If condensation has disappeared entirely, it's time to water. When watering, add only 2-3 tablespoons of water directly to the soil, not the plants.

Mold typically appears when there's excess moisture and poor airflow. This can happen if you've overwatered or if the terrarium is in a dark, cool location. To treat mold: carefully remove the affected leaves or decorations with tweezers, then open the lid for 24-48 hours to let excess moisture escape. Move the terrarium to a brighter location with indirect light. Prevention is key—water sparingly and ensure your terrarium gets adequate light.

Yellow leaves usually indicate overwatering. In closed terrariums, this happens when water is added too frequently. If caught early, you can often save the plant by removing affected leaves with sterile scissors and opening the lid for a few days to let the soil dry out slightly. Always err on the side of underwatering—these are drought-tolerant ecosystems.

Most closed terrariums thrive in bright, indirect light. Direct sunlight can create a greenhouse effect, overheating and damaging plants. Forest and rainforest terrariums prefer medium indirect light, while desert terrariums can handle more sun. North or east-facing windows are ideal. If your only option is a bright south-facing window, place the terrarium a few feet back or use a sheer curtain as protection.

Seasonal Care Tips

Adjust your care routine with the changing seasons

Spring

Plants awaken from winter dormancy. Lightly check soil moisture and resume regular watering schedule if condensation has been absent. Perfect time to remove any dead growth and admire new spring shoots.

Summer

Keep terrariums away from direct sun and hot windows. Air conditioning can dry out homes—check condensation more frequently. Most plants are actively growing, so they may need slightly more water.

Fall

As light decreases, move terrariums closer to windows. Plants slow their growth—reduce watering frequency. This is a good time to do any maintenance pruning before winter dormancy.

Winter

Most plants enter dormancy. Water very sparingly or not at all. Keep away from cold windows and heating vents. Lower light is normal—don't panic if growth slows. Resume normal care in spring.
